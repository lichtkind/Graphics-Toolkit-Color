Developed by: Adobe Systems in 1998 (hence the name).
Purpose: Created as a wider-gamut alternative to sRGB, primarily for professional photographers and print workflows. It was designed to better match the color range of high-quality CMYK printers and inkjet printers at the time.
Key characteristics:Significantly larger gamut than sRGB, especially in greens and cyans.
Covers roughly 50% of the visible colors in CIELAB (compared to sRGB’s ~35%).
Uses a simple gamma of 2.2 (no piecewise function like sRGB).
White point: D65 (same as sRGB).

Main use cases:Professional photography and photo editing when the final output is print.
Still widely supported and accepted by many print labs and stock agencies.
A good compromise: wider than sRGB, but most colors are still displayable on good monitors.

In short: Adobe RGB (1998) was the “serious photographer’s space” for many years — bigger than sRGB without going overboard.2. ProPhoto RGB (also known as ROMM RGB)Developed by: Kodak.
Purpose: Designed for high-end photographic output with the absolute maximum color gamut possible while still being usable in RGB workflows. It is an “output-referred” space meant to preserve as much data as possible from RAW files.
Key characteristics:Extremely large gamut — covers over 90% of real-world surface colors (Pointer’s gamut) and a huge portion of CIELAB.
Actually includes some imaginary colors (colors outside human vision) to allow an even larger real gamut without clipping.
Uses a gamma of 1.8.
White point: D50 (different from sRGB and Adobe RGB, which use D65).

Main use cases:High-end photography and archival work where maximum color fidelity is desired.
Working space for RAW editing in Lightroom/Photoshop when you want to preserve every possible color before converting to a smaller space for output.
Best used in 16-bit mode (because in 8-bit it can cause banding due to the huge gamut).

Important practical note: Because ProPhoto RGB is so large, many colors cannot be displayed on any monitor or printed on any printer. You almost always work in ProPhoto and then convert to a smaller space (Adobe RGB or sRGB) for final output.
